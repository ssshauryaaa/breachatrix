Breach@Trix is a high-intensity cybersecurity challenge where strategy, speed, and technical expertise collide.
Teams compete by taking on opposing cybersecurity roles — Red Team (Attackers) and Blue Team (Defenders) — battling over a deliberately vulnerable website or application.

The objective is to simulate real-world cybersecurity scenarios where attackers attempt to exploit weaknesses while defenders work to secure the system in real time.

Participants

Team Size: 4 students per team

Eligibility: Classes IX – XII

Roles
🔴 Red Team – Attack & Exploit

The Red Team focuses on identifying and exploiting vulnerabilities in the target system.

Responsibilities include:

Discovering vulnerabilities in the given website or application

Performing ethical attacks such as:

SQL Injection

Cross-Site Scripting (XSS)

Authentication bypass

Misconfiguration exploits

Demonstrating proof of exploitation

Scoring points based on successful breaches and their impact

🔵 Blue Team – Defend & Secure

The Blue Team focuses on protecting and securing the system against attacks.

Responsibilities include:

Monitoring and analyzing attack vectors in real time

Identifying exploited vulnerabilities

Patching security flaws quickly

Implementing secure coding practices

Maintaining system stability and availability

Scoring Criteria
Red Team

Points are awarded based on:

Successful exploits

Severity of vulnerabilities discovered

Speed of exploitation

Blue Team

Points are awarded based on:

Effectiveness of security patches

Response time to attacks

System stability and uptime

Bonus Points

Additional points may be awarded for:

Creative attack techniques

Advanced exploitation methods

Clean and well-implemented mitigations

Goal of the Challenge

The goal of Breach@Trix is to provide students with hands-on experience in offensive and defensive cybersecurity, helping them understand how vulnerabilities are discovered, exploited, and mitigated in real-world systems.
